Why LTE‑M and NB‑IoT Matter

Long‑distance asset tracking used to involve trade‑offs: high costs, limited coverage or frequent battery replacements. Two cellular technologies—LTE‑M and NB‑IoT—are changing that paradigm by providing low‑power, wide‑area connectivity. They were developed under the 3GPP standard to allow simple devices to send small data packets over long distances while using minimal energy.

  • LTE‑M (Long Term Evolution for Machines) supports voice (VoLTE), real‑time mobility and up to 1.4 MHz of bandwidth. It is ideal for assets in motion—trucks, rail cars and shipping containers—because devices stay connected as they cross cell towers at highway speeds.
  • NB‑IoT operates on a narrow 180 kHz channel and uses even less power. It excels in stationary or slow‑moving situations such as warehouse pallets, reusable transport containers or cold‑storage units.

These technologies enable trackers with battery lifespans of up to 10 years. By waking only to transmit data at defined intervals, devices conserve energy while still providing continuous monitoring.

Real‑World Deployments

  • Germany’s Deutsche Telekom uses NB‑IoT sensors to track reusable transport packaging, improving recovery rates for low‑value but frequently misplaced assets.
  • Roambee in the United States relies on LTE‑M to monitor pharmaceutical shipments; its devices collect not only location data but also temperature, humidity and light exposure—critical metrics for compliance and quality control.

These examples demonstrate that LPWA technologies are not just proof‑of‑concept. They’re quietly streamlining supply chains today by making lower‑value items part of the data flow.

IoT as the Backbone of Modern Supply Chains

According to Gartner, half of large global companies will integrate AI, advanced analytics and IoT into their supply‑chain operations by 2025. IoT devices deliver transparency by letting companies locate goods, monitor storage conditions and predict arrival times. Key applications include:

  • Asset Tracking and Route Planning – Sensors replace barcodes and manual tracking with continuous location and status updates. Businesses can identify delays in transit and plan alternate routes to keep shipments on schedule.
  • Inventory Visibility – Real‑time monitoring of raw materials, work‑in‑progress and finished goods across facilities helps companies optimize warehouse space and capital. Predictive planning ensures the right stock is in the right place at the right time.
  • Returnable Asset Management – By tagging pallets, crates and shipping containers, businesses can recover missing assets and analyze patterns of theft or misuse.

Collecting time‑stamped data from sensors allows supply‑chain systems to evolve from static status reports to predictive intelligence. Deviations in route, temperature excursions or tampering events can trigger instant alerts, giving companies the ability to act before problems affect product quality.
